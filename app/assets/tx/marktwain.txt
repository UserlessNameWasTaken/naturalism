Mark Twain is a central figure in American realism, whose work laid the groundwork for Naturalism. In Adventures of Huckleberry Finn, he presents a realistic portrayal of society, including issues of race and morality. Twain’s writing often critiques social norms and highlights the influence of environment on behavior. His use of regional dialects adds authenticity to his characters and settings. While not strictly a Naturalist, his emphasis on realism and social critique aligns with the movement. Twain’s work helped shift literature toward more truthful and unidealized representations of life.
