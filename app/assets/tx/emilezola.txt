Émile Zola is widely regarded as the founder and chief theorist of Naturalism, a literary movement that applied scientific principles to fiction. He believed that human behavior is determined by heredity and environment, and he treated his novels almost like social experiments. His massive Rougon-Macquart series explores how family traits and social conditions shape individuals across generations. Works like Germinal vividly depict the harsh realities of coal miners, emphasizing economic struggle and environmental influence. Zola’s writing often highlights how individuals have limited control over their fate. His influence defined Naturalism as a movement grounded in determinism and realism.
